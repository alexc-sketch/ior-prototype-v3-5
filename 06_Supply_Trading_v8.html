The Challenge

Australia's fuel supply chain is more vulnerable than most realise.

Dependence on imported fuel, limited domestic refining capacity, and ageing infrastructure leave Australian industry exposed. IOR was built to change that.

Supply Vulnerability

Import dependency creates critical exposure

Australia imports the majority of its refined fuel. A single disruption to a major shipping route or refinery can cascade into national shortages within days — with no domestic buffer to absorb the shock.

Infrastructure Gaps

Strategic storage is concentrated in too few locations

Most bulk fuel storage is clustered around capital city ports. Regional and remote operations — mining, agriculture, defence — have limited access to strategic reserves and are first to feel supply disruptions.

Sovereign Capability

Domestic refining capacity has been steadily eroded

Australia's domestic refining footprint has shrunk dramatically over the past two decades. IOR's Eromanga Refinery is one of the last remaining sovereign fuel manufacturers — producing high-quality diesel in the heart of the outback since 1984.

FAQs

Supply & Trading — common questions.

The Eromanga Refinery is IOR's sovereign fuel manufacturing facility located approximately 1,000km west of Brisbane in outback Queensland. Operating since 1984, it produces up to 1,250 barrels per day of high-quality diesel, including the unique Eromanga UMF mining fuel — the lowest-particulate diesel mining fuel on the Australian market, with less than 10ppm sulphur content.

IOR operates two strategic import terminals: Lytton Terminal at the Port of Brisbane, Queensland (110ML capacity, constructed 2023, largest diesel-only terminal in QLD), and Port Bonython Terminal located 25km north of Whyalla, South Australia (81ML capacity across three 27ML tanks, largest diesel storage facility in SA). Both terminals operate around the clock and feature deep-water berths for bulk fuel imports.

IOR's two import terminals provide a combined storage capacity of 191 megalitres — 110ML at Lytton Terminal (Port of Brisbane) and 81ML at Port Bonython Terminal (South Australia). Both terminals feature 2,400 litre-per-minute loading gantries and deep-water berths for bulk fuel imports.
